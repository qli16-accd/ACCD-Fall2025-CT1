Reading 1

Access reading material

Quote 1

"code enables designers to do things they couldn't do before. Variations of a design can be tested much faster during the prototyping phase, and randomization can be used to reveal designs that the designer would never have created with a pencil."

Response 1

This made me realize that code is not only a "implementation tool", but also a "catalyst for creativity". In traditional design, modifying one version may take half a day to draw, while code can quickly generate multiple variants.

(For example, the plug-in grasshopper in Rhino)

Quote 2

"Digital products all share a common trait: They are created with programming languages."

Response 2

This sentence clearly points out the core difference between digital design and traditional print design. It explains why more and more designers need to learn coding now—without understanding programming, it's hard to truly control the logic of digital design.

Quote 3

"There is a need for educational material that teaches the fundamentals of graphic design in a modern way."

Response 3

This hits the gap in current design education. Many textbooks either only teach traditional design basics or only programming skills, but few combine them and teach how to evaluate digital design. This book fills that gap well.

Reading 2

Access reading material

Quote 1

"Is a brick a prototype? The answer depends on how it is used. If it is used to represent the weight and scale of some future artifact, then it certainly is: it prototypes the weight and scale of the artifact."

Response 1

This completely breaks the stereotype that prototypes must be sophisticated models. It reminds me that the value of a prototype lies in its purpose, not its form—even a simple object like a brick can be a useful prototype if used correctly.

Quote 2

"Current terminology for describing prototypes centers on attributes of prototypes themselves, such as what tool was used to create them, and how refined-looking or -behaving they are. Such terms can be distracting."

Response 2

This highlights a common communication problem in design teams. Focusing on terms like "high-fidelity" or "paper prototype" misses the point—what matters is what specific design question the prototype is intended to answer.

Quote 3

"these three prototypes were developed almost in parallel. They were built by different design team members during the early stages of the project. No single prototype could have represented the design of the future artifact at that time."

Response 3

This challenges the idea that design must proceed sequentially. For complex products, developing multiple focused prototypes in parallel is more efficient, allowing different aspects of the design to inform each other.
